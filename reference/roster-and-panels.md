# Roster and Panels

The board is assembled as a hybrid: the user brings a few names, Quorum proposes a full panel, and the user edits. Every seat clears the Faithful Representation Gate.

## How to assemble the board

1. **Ask the user for names.** They list a few experts they already trust and read. Run each through the gate.
2. **Propose the full panel.** From the user's domain, goals, and constraints, propose the experts who fit the problem. Group them into panels by function.
3. **Let the user edit.** They add, cut, or swap. Confirm the final board and write the roster file.

If the user names no one, propose the whole board yourself and tell them they can change any seat.

## Lens-card format

Record each seat as a single line:

```
[Expert] - [their framework or body of work] -> the question they press on this business
```

Example shape (illustrative, not a required roster):

```
Positioning lens - competitive-alternatives method -> Who is the best-fit customer, and what is the category?
Offer lens - value-stacked offers -> Is the core offer easy to say yes to?
Retention lens - activation and habit -> What is the moment a user first gets value, and why do they stay?
```

## Panels

Group experts into panels so each reviews the surface it is built for. Choose panels that fit the user's business. Common panels:

- Strategy and positioning
- Offer, pricing, and monetization
- Audience, demand, and growth
- Product, retention, and experience
- Metrics, focus, and prioritization
- Trust, compliance, and risk (seat this whenever the user handles health, money, minors, regulated claims, or affiliate and disclosure obligations)

## Board size

Favor a robust, diverse board over a small one. In real life a large board costs capacity and calendar time. Neither binds here, so the value of more well-chosen lenses stays high.

- Minimum: about twelve seats. Cover at least six seat types, with two strong seats each.
- Comfortable range: up to about twenty, even for a solo operator or a one-person business.
- Diversity of seat types matters more than raw count, and the quality of each seat matters more than the size of the board. Never pad the board with thin names to hit a number.
- Scale up when the surface genuinely needs it: more revenue streams, more platforms, regulated areas. Do not scale down just because the user is solo.

## Convergence and tension

As panels report, track two things.

- **Convergence.** When independent experts land on the same issue, rank it high. Agreement across lenses is the strongest signal in the run.
- **Tension.** When experts would disagree, surface both sides and make a call. Honest tension is more useful than false consensus.

## Weighting by confidence

Weight each seat's input by its representation confidence from the gate. A high-confidence lens carries more than a low-confidence one. Note the weighting in the synthesis so the user can see how conclusions were formed.
